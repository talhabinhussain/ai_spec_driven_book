## What is Physical AI?

Physical AI represents a paradigm shift from traditional digital AI models to systems that operate in the physical world. Unlike purely digital AI systems that process abstract data, Physical AI systems must interact with real-world physics, sensorimotor contingencies, and dynamic environments.

### Key Characteristics

- **Embodied**: The AI system exists within a physical body with sensors and actuators
- **Situated**: The system operates in a real or simulated physical environment
- **Dynamic**: The system must respond to continuous changes in its environment
- **Interactive**: The system learns through physical interaction with its environment

## Digital-to-Physical Transition Challenges

Moving from digital AI models to physical systems introduces several challenges:

1. **Reality Gap**: Differences between simulation and real-world behavior
2. **Latency Constraints**: Physical systems require real-time responses
3. **Safety Considerations**: Physical systems must operate safely in human environments
4. **Uncertainty Management**: Physical sensors and actuators have inherent noise and uncertainty
5. **Embodiment Constraints**: Physical form factors limit possible actions and interactions

## Sensor Grounding and Perception Loops

Physical AI systems rely on sensor grounding to connect abstract concepts with physical reality. This involves:

- **Perception-Action Loops**: Continuous cycles of sensing, processing, and acting
- **Sensor Fusion**: Combining data from multiple sensors for robust perception
- **Active Perception**: Controlling sensors to gather the most relevant information
- **Learning from Interaction**: Improving performance through physical experience

## Physics Constraints and Robot-Environment Coupling

Physical systems must respect the laws of physics, which introduces constraints:

- **Dynamics**: How forces affect motion and stability
- **Kinematics**: Geometric relationships between joints and end-effectors
- **Contact Mechanics**: How robots interact with objects and surfaces
- **Energy Efficiency**: Managing power consumption in physical systems

## State Representation in Physical Systems

Unlike digital systems that can maintain perfect state, physical systems must manage:

- **Uncertainty**: Sensor noise and actuator variability
- **Partial Observability**: Limited sensor information
- **Temporal Dynamics**: State changes over time due to physics
- **Embodied Constraints**: State is coupled to physical form and capabilities
